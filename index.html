  1. Accuracy: This site provides accurate information with plenty clear examples. The content emphasizes the importance of data accuracy and the potential for misleading data. It encourages readers to be critical and not take data at face value.

  4. Clarity: The content is generally clear, with straightforward language and minimal jargon. It is focused on conveying a clear message about data integrity and potential pitfalls.
  5. Conciseness: The content is relatively concise and does not use unnecessary wordiness. It gets to the point and avoids unnecessary elaboration.
  6. Conversational and Smoothly Flowing Prose: The content is written in a conversational tone and uses contractions when appropriate. It also starts sentences with conjunctions to create a conversational tone.

  9. Positive Voice: The content encourages a sceptical but positive approach to data, which aligns with the principle of maintaining a constructive and open-minded perspective.
  10. Strong Nouns and Verbs: The content uses strong nouns and verbs effectively. For example, "Instead of accepting data at face value, try digging a bit deeper to see the true picture." This eliminates the need for excessive adverbs and adjectives.
  11. Sentence Variety: The content varies sentence structure effectively. It avoids repetitive sentence beginnings and incorporates both short and longer sentences to maintain reader engagement.
  12. Simple Words: The content predominantly uses simple and recognizable words. It avoids showy or overly complex vocabulary.
  13. Shorter Paragraphs: The content utilizes relatively short paragraphs, making it more reader-friendly and digestible.
  14. Tone and Voice: The tone is appropriate for the subject matter, maintaining a sceptical yet constructive perspective on data. It adapts to the context effectively.

Why Children Need to be taught Data Literacy

Data Literate Kids

Boosting Students Literacy

When comparing images from websites on a similar topic, particularly those aimed at explaining data concepts to a young audience, it's crucial to consider how the imagery aligns with the needs and comprehension levels of children. In this case, the images encountered are primarily tailored for adults (educators and parents) which can lead to a disconnect when children are the end-users.

For children, the effectiveness of an image is often measured by how well it can translate complex ideas into simple, engaging visuals that capture their imagination and aid in retention. Images that work well for adults, such as real-world photographs or abstract illustrations, may not hold the same appeal or clarity for a younger demographic. In contrast, images that can tell a story, with characters they can relate to or scenarios they find familiar, are more likely to resonate with children and facilitate learning.

These images, while aesthetically pleasing and designed to break the monotony of text, fall short in terms of educational utility for children. They don't necessarily contribute to a better grasp of the material, as they are not designed with the cognitive and perceptual abilities of children in mind. For instance, a photograph of child behind a laptop might be relevant to an educator-focused site, but it doesn't engage a child who is trying to understand data through play and exploration.

To make the images more suitable for a child's learning experience, it's important to pivot towards visuals that are character-driven, vibrant, and narrative-based. These could take the form of colourful cartoons or interactive diagrams that allow children to see the progression of a story or concept as they interact with it. This approach not only makes the learning process more enjoyable but also ensures that the imagery is directly contributing to the educational goals of the website.

While the existing images may enhance the visual appeal of the websites but miss the mark in effectiveness for children. A child-centric approach to imagery would necessitate a shift towards more illustrative, interactive, and story-based visuals that align with the way children process information and stay engaged with educational content.

Assessing the Quality of Images on my Website

Optimising images for a child-oriented website like "Digit Detective" involves balancing visual appeal with technical performance. Considering the Fidelity Criteria, aiming for 'Fine' quality images ensures that the visuals are engaging without being unnecessarily large in file size, which could impact site loading times. Vector images are an excellent choice for this type of site due to their scalability and clear graphics, perfect for line art, illustrations with text, and images requiring transparency. Utilising PNG format for these vector images takes advantage of lossless compression, preserving visual quality while often reducing file size for faster load times.

For the web, optimising images also includes proper sizing and resolution. Since the target audience is children, who may be viewing the site on various devices, images should be responsive, ensuring they look sharp on high-resolution displays while being scaled down appropriately for smaller screens. Overall, the strategy would be to create a colourful, visually rich experience that aligns with the 'Fine' quality standard, using well-optimised vector graphics to ensure the site is responsive and loads quickly across all devices. This approach supports an engaging, educational experience without compromising on speed or accessibility.

WAVE Web Accessibility Evaluation Tool

Upon evaluating the accessibility of loharris.com, the web accessibility evaluation tool flagged several issues. Firstly, the website fails to specify the language, which is crucial for screen readers and automatic translation services. The absence of first-level headings (which occurred 77 times) hinders navigation for those using assistive technologies and diminishes the document's structural clarity. Additionally, the site contains elements with insufficient contrast, posing challenges for users with visual impairments. There are also issues with missing ARIA (Accessible Rich Internet Applications) references, leading to descriptions not being properly relayed to users. The presence of empty links creates confusion, as their function or purpose is unclear to screen reader and keyboard users. Finally, the website has numerous redundant links, which result in unnecessary repetition and navigation complications for these users.

WebAIM Contrast Checker

The use of contrast and colour plays an essential role in making web content accessible. It is imperative for all users, particularly those with visual impairments, to be able to discern the content presented on a webpage. Contrast refers to the difference in "luminance" or brightness that makes an object (such as text) distinguishable from another (such as its background). This difference is quantified as a ratio, with the scale ranging from 1:1 to 21:1. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) established by Web Accessibility in Mind (WebAIM) stipulate that text and images of text must have a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 to ensure readability. However, an evaluation using the WebAIM contrast checker indicated that a significant portion of the text on the Lo Harris website does not meet this requisite contrast ratio, potentially compromising text accessibility.

Readability Test Tool

The readability test indicated that the website's content is clear enough for comprehension by users as young as 12 to 13 years old, as revealed by a score of 63.8. While the site is not specifically aimed at a younger demographic, the findings affirm that its content is easily accessible and understandable to a broad spectrum of users.

Reflecting on accessibility

Keyboard Navigation:

It is essential to ensure that all interactive elements, such as links, buttons, form fields, and media controls, can be accessed using the keyboard alone. This means that users should be able to navigate through every part of the website using tab keys, arrow keys, and other standard keyboard shortcuts. Users with motor disabilities may find using a mouse challenging or impossible, and those with visual impairments often rely on screen readers that are navigated by keyboard. When developing my site in MAD(B) I need to provide focus indicators, provide a logical navigation order and use links and buttons appropriately.

Useful Resource for next semester - WebAIM: Keyboard Accessibility

Avoid Seizure-Inducing Content:

Content that flashes, blinks, or flickers can pose a serious health risk to users with photosensitive epilepsy. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) recommend avoiding flashing that is more frequent than three times per second to prevent seizures. When developing my site in MAD(B) I need to be wary of mouse-over effects, avoid red flashing and comply with WCAG guidelines.

Useful resource for next semester - MDN Web Docs: Seizure Disorders

Testing with Users:

Including people with disabilities in the user testing process is invaluable. First-hand feedback from users with a range of disabilities can provide insights that automated tools cannot. Engaging with this diverse group can help identify practical issues and barriers that might not be obvious otherwise.
